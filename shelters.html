Start your own organization

A Shelter
ASPCA's National Shelter Outreach has great tips to help you get started!

  • Ensure there is a gap in community needs. Determine issues in the local animal welfare industry and what goals are being failed to reach. Would your organization be successful? Would it benefit your community? If there are more than one shelter in your community, would adding another one be an advantage or disadvantage in your community?
  • Do your research and know exactly what you're getting into and the best way to approach the task.
  • Gain the proper experience in the realm of sheltering and rescueing pets beyond cleaning the kennels and walking the dogs.
  • Develop a team who you trust and know are loyal to the goal of the product. It takes an entrepreneurial spirit to start up an organization.
  • Visit other shelters. Get to know what methods of implementation work and don't work to create a successful org.
  • Know what type of shelter you want. Waht animals will you rescue? Just dogs? Cats too? Birds? Bunnies? Specific breeds? This is important to recognize components like budget, networks, connections, physical space needed, experts needs and other staff.
  • Create a mission statement, a plan and FUND. Running a shelter is a business. Hold events and open up to donations.
  • Toot your horn! Have a GRAND opening to promote and spread the word of your org!

A Breed-Specific Rescue
Purebred dog rescue organizations grew out of the concern of “fanciers” of various breeds to find homes for unwanted, abandoned, stray, mistreated or neglected dogs of those breeds. Breed specific rescue groups are made up of dedicated volunteers who house, care for and carefully place purebreds in new, hopefully permanent, homes. For prospective dog owners who are interested in a particular breed, this type of rescue group provides an alternative to breeders or pet shops. An important plus is that these rescue organizations do not contribute to the companion overpopulation problem.

Breed specific rescue groups work hand in hand with and complement the work of animal shelters. Many shelters maintain a list of breed groups in their area, and when a purebred is received (shelters report that between 20 percent and 30 percent of dogs received are purebreds), call for assistance with placement. As purebred rescue groups remove “their” breed from a shelter, they free up a run and buy time for another dog. Additionally, many rescue groups take dogs who might be considered less adoptable by some shelters, such as dogs who are older or have special needs.
